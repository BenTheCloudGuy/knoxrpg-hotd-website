# The History of Faerûn & the Halls of the Damned

## The Dawn Ages (−35,000 DR to −30,000 DR)

Long before the rise of elves, dwarves, or humanity, the world of **Toril** belonged to beings later remembered only in myth as the **Creator Races**, known in ancient elven records as the *Iqua-Tel'Quessir* — *The First Peoples*. These primordial civilizations shaped the earliest ages of Faerûn when the world was still young and raw with untamed magic. During this era the continents themselves were still settling from the chaos of creation. Vast elemental forces churned beneath the surface of the world, and the **Weave of magic had not yet fully stabilized**. Storms of arcane energy roamed the skies, and portals to other planes appeared unpredictably across the land. It was within this volatile world that the Creator Races rose to dominance.

During the Early Dawn Ages the world was still closely tied to the **Elemental Chaos**. Powerful primordial spirits walked the land freely, shaping storms, volcanoes, and oceans according to their will. Entire mountain ranges could rise or collapse in a single generation. Seas boiled with elemental fire. Massive forests grew for thousands of miles before burning in magical infernos. The gods watched the world from afar, but their influence was not yet absolute. Many regions of Toril remained under the sway of elemental powers and ancient cosmic forces. This unstable balance would not last.

### The Great Empires of the Creator Races

The most powerful civilizations of the Dawn Ages included. **The Dragons**, whose ancient empires ruled vast skies and mountain ranges. Their earliest broods were immensely powerful, closer to primordial forces than the dragons of later ages. Many claimed descent from the dragon god **Io**, and their wars reshaped entire regions of the world. **The Giants**, who built colossal kingdoms across mountain ranges and deep valleys. Guided by the teachings of **Annam the All-Father**, the giants constructed enormous fortresses and stone cities that rivaled mountains in scale. **The Aboleths**, ancient aberrations from the deepest oceans. Their empires existed long before surface civilizations, and their psychic dominion stretched through vast undersea kingdoms and subterranean lakes. **Primordial beings and elder aberrations**, creatures of alien origin that existed beyond mortal understanding. Some were remnants of cosmic forces from before the gods themselves shaped the world. These civilizations warred constantly for dominion over Toril. Their battles shaped mountains, redirected rivers, and shattered entire regions of the world. Yet despite their conflicts, they also created wonders—vast cities of crystal and stone, enormous temples to forgotten powers, and magical constructs whose purposes have long been lost to time. Fragments of these ancient structures still lie buried beneath Faerûn, often mistaken for ruins of later civilizations.

### The Dawn War

During the earliest ages of Toril, when the **Creator Races** ruled the world, the gods themselves became embroiled in a conflict that reshaped the cosmos. This war—remembered in fragmentary myth as **The Dawn War**—was fought between the divine powers of the heavens and the primordial forces that sought to dominate the newborn world. In this era the boundaries between the planes were thin, and powerful elemental beings known as **Primordials** roamed freely across Toril. These titanic entities embodied the raw forces of creation—earth, fire, storm, and sea—and many sought to shape the world according to their own chaotic visions. The gods opposed them. For centuries the world was caught between these competing powers. Mountains rose and shattered, oceans boiled, and entire regions of Toril were scarred by battles between divine and primordial forces. Yet the most terrible threat did not come from the Primordials. It came from one of the gods themselves. 

Among the younger gods was **Tharizdun**, a deity whose mind was devoted to understanding the deepest mysteries of existence. Where other gods shaped worlds and guided mortals, Tharizdun sought knowledge hidden beneath creation itself. His studies eventually led him beyond the boundaries of the known planes. There he encountered something that no god was meant to perceive—an alien power that existed **outside the order of reality**. What he saw shattered his mind. Tharizdun became convinced that existence itself was flawed. In his twisted reasoning, the cosmos could only achieve perfection through absolute control and total destruction of chaos—ironically through the very forces he had uncovered. Driven mad by this revelation, Tharizdun turned against the divine host.

Using forbidden knowledge drawn from the void beyond the planes, Tharizdun began shaping a new realm: **The Abyss**. Unlike the structured outer planes, the Abyss was a place of pure chaos and endless corruption. From this nightmarish realm Tharizdun created the first **demons**, monstrous beings forged from elemental chaos and divine hatred. These creatures became the soldiers of his rebellion. Tharizdun also created a powerful avatar to act as his will beyond the Abyss: **Shorthagot — the Harbinger of Ruin.**

Where Tharizdun represented cosmic madness, Shorthagot embodied destruction and corruption within the mortal world. The rebellion of Tharizdun turned the Dawn War into a catastrophe that threatened all existence. Gods clashed with gods. Primordials fought alongside demons. Creator Race civilizations found themselves caught in the crossfire of battles between beings of unimaginable power. Entire regions of Toril were devastated by these conflicts. Some ancient ruins discovered today may be remnants of cities destroyed during these divine wars.

At last the gods realized that Tharizdun could not be slain without risking the collapse of reality itself. Instead they chose to imprison him. Through an immense act of divine magic, the gods bound Tharizdun within the depths of the Abyss itself, sealing him behind layers of cosmic chains and planar barriers.From that moment he became known as: **The Chained God.**. Though his physical form was imprisoned, his consciousness remained active. From within his prison he whispered to mortals across the planes, slowly gathering cults dedicated to freeing him. His avatar **Shorthagot** survived the war and continued to act in his master's name, seeking ways to weaken the divine prison.

When the Dawn War finally ended, the world of Toril was forever changed. The Primordials were banished or imprisoned. The gods took a greater role in shaping the world. And the Creator Races—dragons, giants, and ancient aberrations—continued their struggles for dominance across a wounded but stabilizing world. Yet the scars of the war never truly faded.Deep beneath mountains and oceans lie remnants of that ancient conflict—ruins, artifacts, and forgotten powers tied to the madness of Tharizdun. Many sages believe that some of these relics may still hold the power to weaken the chains that bind the Chained God. If that prison were ever broken, the Dawn War might begin again.

### The Tearfall

The end of the Dawn Ages came in a cataclysm remembered in ancient records as **The Tearfall**. Without warning, a vast meteor storm descended upon Toril. Thousands of celestial bodies struck the world in a devastating bombardment that reshaped entire continents. Mountains were shattered. Oceans boiled. Entire civilizations vanished in a single night.

Many scholars believe the meteor storm originated from the heavens beyond Realmspace, while others claim it was an act of divine intervention meant to end the unchecked dominion of the Creator Races. Whatever its origin, the effects were catastrophic. The Tearfall marked the **collapse of the great primordial empires**. The dragons and giants survived the catastrophe but were weakened and fragmented. The aboleths retreated into the deepest oceans and subterranean waters, while many other ancient beings vanished entirely.

In the centuries that followed, Toril began to stabilize. The elemental chaos receded, the Weave grew more ordered, and the stage was set for the rise of new civilizations. Though the great empires of the Creator Races fell, their legacy did not disappear. Across Faerûn, explorers still discover ruins dating back to the Dawn Ages, including: Cyclopean stone cities buried beneath mountains, Caverns carved by beings larger than dragons, Artifacts forged from materials unknown to modern magic, Structures whose purposes defy explanation  


## The First Flowering (−24,000 DR to −12,000 DR)

Following the devastation of the Tearfall and the collapse of the primordial empires, a new civilization rose to prominence across Faerûn: the **Tel'Quessir**, the elves. This era became known in elven histories as **The First Flowering**, a time when elven culture, magic, and artistry spread across the continent in harmony with the natural world. The forests of Faerûn became the cradle of elven civilization, and vast woodland kingdoms emerged beneath ancient canopies untouched by time. During this period, the elves developed a philosophy unlike any other civilization before or since. Rather than dominate the land, the Tel'Quessir sought to **live in balance with it**, shaping their cities from living trees, crystal structures, and carefully woven magic.

### The Rise of the Elven Realms

Across Faerûn, powerful elven realms began to take shape. These powerful groups ruled Toril for more than 10,000 years.. Among the most significant were:

- **Aryvandaar** — A mighty sun elf kingdom that dominated the western forests of Faerûn.
- **Shantel Othreier** — A vast woodland empire in the region now known as the High Forest.
- **Illefarn** — A northern elven realm that would later influence the cultures of the Sword Coast.
- **The Forest of Cormanthor** — A sacred woodland that would eventually become the heart of elven civilization in eastern Faerûn.

Within the deep forest of **Cormanthor**, early elven settlements flourished. These communities would later evolve into one of the greatest cities in elven history: **Myth Drannor**, though its founding would not occur for many thousands of years. During the early centuries of the First Flowering, the elven gods guided their people to the creation of **Evermeet**, a mystical island far to the west across the Trackless Sea. Evermeet was shaped through powerful high magic rituals performed by elven High Mages. The island became both a refuge and a spiritual homeland for the Tel'Quessir—a place where elven culture could endure even if the mainland realms fell. For many elves, Evermeet was seen as the closest place on Toril to the divine realm of **Arvandor**, the home of the Seldarine.

Perhaps the greatest achievement of the First Flowering was the development of **Elven High Magic**, a form of spellcasting far more powerful and complex than the magic practiced by later civilizations. High Magic was not the work of a single wizard but of **circles of High Mages**, whose combined will allowed them to shape reality itself. Through these rituals the elves created: **Mythals**, vast magical fields that protected entire cities, Living forests shaped through arcane growth, Dimensional wards that kept hostile creatures from entering elven lands, Permanent magical structures woven directly into the land. Many of the most powerful magical locations in Faerûn were created during this era.

### The War Beneath the World

Despite the beauty of the First Flowering, the elves were not without enemies. Deep beneath Faerûn, the **phaerimm**—ancient and malevolent beings of immense magical power—began expanding their influence through the Underdark. These creatures possessed a natural hatred of surface civilizations and sought to corrupt or destroy any rival power. The phaerimm unleashed magical plagues and destructive spells against elven realms, often using enslaved creatures to wage war on the surface. In response, the elves formed alliances with many other races:

- **Treants and forest spirits**
- **Fey creatures of the Feywild**
- **Early dwarven kingdoms**
- **Nature spirits tied to the land**

Together they fought back against the subterranean threat. These conflicts would continue for millennia and would eventually contribute to the rise of other magical civilizations, including **Netheril**, which later fought its own terrible war against the phaerimm. For many thousands of years the First Flowering was remembered as a **golden age of peace, art, and magic**. Elven music, sculpture, and poetry flourished. Cities hidden beneath forest canopies became legendary for their beauty. Trade routes formed between distant elven realms, and knowledge flowed freely across the continent. Yet beneath this harmony, tensions were slowly building. Pride and rivalry between the great elven houses began to grow. Political alliances became fragile, and ideological divisions deepened between different elven cultures. These fractures would eventually erupt into one of the most devastating series of wars in elven history. The conflicts that followed would become known as **The Crown Wars.**. The Five devastating conflicts between elven nations scarred the face of Faerûn. The Crown Wars led to the fall of many great elven kingdoms, the corruption of the Ilythiiri (who would become the drow), and the retreat of elven power from the surface world.


## The Rise of Netheril (−3,859 DR to −339 DR)

Following the long decline of the ancient elven empires after the Crown Wars, a new power began to rise in the northern reaches of Faerûn. In the lands that would later become the **Great Desert of Anauroch**, scattered human tribes discovered fragments of lost magical knowledge buried beneath ancient ruins. From these discoveries emerged one of the greatest arcane civilizations in the history of Toril: **The Empire of Netheril.**.  Over the course of nearly three millennia, Netheril grew from a loose confederation of tribes into a vast magical empire whose power rivaled that of the ancient elves. The true beginning of Netheril’s rise came with the discovery of the **Nether Scrolls**, ancient magical texts believed to predate even the Crown Wars. These artifacts contained profound arcane knowledge that allowed human mages to advance their understanding of magic at an unprecedented pace. Through study of the Scrolls, Netherese arcanists developed new forms of spellcraft that surpassed anything humanity had previously achieved. Within only a few centuries, Netheril became the most magically advanced human civilization ever to exist.

### The Structure of Netherese Society

Netherese society became rigidly divided between those who wielded arcane power and those who did not. The empire was ruled by **archwizards known as arcanists**, whose magical abilities granted them immense authority. Society divided into three primary classes:

**The Arcanists**: Powerful wizards who controlled the government, magical research, and the floating enclaves of Netheril. Their influence was absolute, and many viewed themselves as beings above ordinary mortals.
**The High Citizens**: Merchants, scholars, priests, and lesser nobles who served the magical elite. Though not all possessed magical talent, they lived in great cities and enjoyed wealth and influence.
**The Low Netherese**: Farmers, laborers, and craftsmen who supported the empire’s vast infrastructure. Most lived in the towns and villages scattered across Netheril’s surface lands.


The greatest achievement of Netherese magic was the creation of **flying cities known as enclaves**. Over time, the division between these classes became extreme. The floating enclaves that housed the arcanists became increasingly isolated from the lives of ordinary people. Using immense magical engines called **mythallars**, the Netherese lifted entire cities into the sky. A mythallar was a colossal magical sphere capable of generating enormous arcane energy. This power sustained the floating cities and allowed Netherese arcanists to cast spells far beyond the normal limits of magic. These cities floated miles above the earth, visible from the ground as glittering jewels in the sky.

**Thultanthar** – The City of Shade, one of the most powerful and enduring enclaves.
**Xinlenal** – A center of magical research and experimentation.
**Anauria** – A wealthy enclave known for its grand libraries and academies.
**Sakkors** – A military stronghold that guarded Netheril’s borders.


Netheril was governed by a council of the most powerful archwizards in the empire, often referred to as **The Twelve**. Though the membership changed over centuries, these individuals controlled the greatest magical resources and political authority in Netheril. Their rule was often unstable, as rival arcanists constantly competed for greater knowledge and power. Political intrigue, magical experimentation, and personal ambition defined Netherese governance.

Netheril’s laws reflected the dominance of arcane power. Magical ability was considered the ultimate measure of authority. Arcanists possessed the right to command lesser citizens, and many enforced their rule through magical constructs and summoned creatures. While these laws allowed magical innovation to flourish, they also led to reckless experimentation that would eventually doom the empire. Some of the most notable Netherese legal principles included:

- **Arcane Supremacy:** Arcanists possessed legal authority over non-magical citizens.
- **Magical Experimentation:** Research was rarely restricted, even when dangerous.
- **Enclave Autonomy:** Each floating city governed itself under its ruling archwizard.


Netherese magic expanded rapidly during this era. Arcanists developed spells of staggering power, including magic capable of altering weather, reshaping landscapes, and controlling entire regions of the world. Some archmages even began experimenting with forces believed to exist beyond the **Weave of magic itself**. It was during this period that several secret magical facilities were constructed beneath the mountains and deserts of Netheril. One such facility would later become known as **Thul Avenar**, a hidden research complex where Netherese scholars attempted to harness a mysterious power known in ancient texts as **Primordial Aether**. Few records of these experiments survived the empire’s fall.

### The Fall of Netheril

The greatest—and most dangerous—archwizard Netheril ever produced was **Karsus**. A prodigy whose magical skill surpassed that of his peers, Karsus believed the gods themselves had become a limitation on mortal progress. As Netheril faced growing threats from the **phaerimm**, Karsus sought a solution that would ensure the survival of his empire forever.
His answer was a spell unlike any other ever attempted. 

In −339 DR, Karsus completed the most powerful spell ever created: **Karsus’s Avatar**. The spell was designed to steal the divine power of a god, allowing Karsus to become a deity himself. His target was **Mystryl**, the goddess who governed the Weave of magic. When the spell succeeded, the consequences were catastrophic. For a brief moment Karsus became a god—but he could not control the immense power he had seized. The strain shattered the Weave itself. Mystryl sacrificed herself to restore magic to the world, but the damage was already done.

When magic failed across the world, the floating enclaves of Netheril immediately lost the power that kept them aloft. One by one the great cities of the sky fell from the heavens.
Entire populations were destroyed as their cities crashed into the earth. The empire collapsed within a matter of hours. In the aftermath, the once fertile lands of Netheril slowly transformed into the vast desert now known as **Anauroch**.

The destruction of the floating cities was only the most visible consequence of Netheril’s fall. Far beneath the mountains and deserts of the empire, many of Netheril’s hidden research complexes were conducting experiments that pushed far beyond the limits of the Weave. One such facility was **Thul Avenar**, a secret Netherese research complex buried deep beneath the mountains northeast of what would one day become Barovia. There, a circle of Netherese arcanists had begun studying a force known in their records as **Primordial Aether**—a cosmic power believed to predate both the gods and the Weave itself. Their experiments centered on a vast arcane construct known as: **The Great Machine.**

This device was not a single artifact but an immense arcane superstructure designed to channel Primordial Aether through a network of dimensional anchors and magical conduits. The Netherese believed it could provide limitless power—greater even than a mythallar. But the Machine had another purpose. During their experiments, the Netherese scholars encountered an ancient entity trapped within the aetheric currents that flowed beyond the Weave. Whether Shorthagot was a primordial god, a fragment of an elder power, or something even older remains unknown. What is known is that contact with the entity slowly corrupted the Netherese researchers. Shorthagot promised them knowledge beyond the reach of the gods and power capable of surpassing even the mythallars of the flying cities. Under its influence, the Netherese began altering the Great Machine so that it would no longer merely channel Primordial Aether—but open a gateway through which Shorthagot itself could enter the world.


When the Weave collapsed during Karsus’s Folly, the arcane network sustaining the Great Machine destabilized. The catastrophic surge of magical energy nearly tore open the gateway the Netherese had been constructing. In the chaos that followed, the remaining researchers realized the horror they had nearly unleashed. Using the last remnants of their High Netherese rituals, they performed a desperate containment working. The Machine was shattered into multiple arcane components, and its power was sealed behind dimensional locks.
These locks became known in later ages as **The Keys of the Great Machine**. The corrupted research complex of **Thul Avenar** was buried beneath the mountains as the magical backlash collapsed entire cavern systems. Many of the surviving Netherese records were lost or destroyed. The few surviving texts spoke only in fragmented warnings: *“Let the Machine remain silent, lest the Sleeper awaken.”*

### Legacy of the Netherese

Though the empire was destroyed, Netheril’s legacy remains scattered across Faerûn. Buried beneath desert sands and mountain ranges lie the ruins of their cities and laboratories.  any of these sites contain artifacts of immense power, some of which are still poorly understood by modern scholars. Among the most mysterious remnants of Netherese experimentation are ancient machines, magical constructs, and planar devices whose true purpose has long been forgotten. Some sages believe that certain Netherese experiments attempted to harness forces older than the Weave itself. If such devices still exist, they may hold powers capable of reshaping the world once again.

In the centuries following Netheril’s fall, knowledge of the Great Machine faded into myth. Most scholars believed the Netherese ruins contained only relics of lost magical experiments. But hidden cults, secret orders, and ancient beings continued searching for fragments of the Machine and the Keys that controlled it. For they believed that if the Machine were restored, its power could reshape the world. Or destroy it.

### The Early Von Zarovich Era -300 - 1DR

In the centuries following the fall of Netheril, the lands of the northern frontier remained fractured and lawless. Scattered tribes, warlords, and petty kingdoms struggled for dominance across the harsh plains and mountain valleys between the **Galena Mountains** and the **Giantspire Mountains**. Into this chaos emerged the warrior clan **Von Zarovich**.Originally a northern warband of formidable reputation, the Von Zarovich family rose under the leadership of **Viktor Von Zarovich**, a brilliant strategist and relentless conqueror. Through a combination of military discipline and calculated diplomacy, Viktor united many of the competing tribes and settlements under a single banner. Within a few decades, the Von Zarovich dominion stretched across vast territories including what would later become **Damara, Vaasa, Narfell, and Impiltur**. 

Viktor declared the founding of a new kingdom and established his capital at the fortified city of **Morov**, built upon the ruins of an ancient Netherese outpost. From this seat of power the Von Zarovich rulers imposed order upon a region long plagued by instability. Though their rule was stern and often uncompromising, it brought the first real stability the region had known since the fall of Netheril. Roads were built. Fortresses rose along the borders. Trade began to flow once more across the eastern plains. For the first time in centuries, the lands of Damara began to resemble a unified realm.

The task of transforming a conquered territory into a functioning kingdom fell to **King Barov I Von Zarovich**, the son of Viktor. Where his father had been a conqueror, Barov proved to be a capable ruler and statesman. Barov strengthened the foundations of the young kingdom through a combination of military reforms and diplomatic alliances. Tribal leaders who once fought against the Von Zarovich rule were gradually incorporated into the kingdom’s nobility, granted lands and titles in exchange for loyalty. During Barov’s reign several major initiatives reshaped Damaran society:
- **Fortified Cities** were constructed throughout the eastern plains to secure trade routes.
- **River trade networks** connected Damara with southern kingdoms.
- **Standing armies** replaced the tribal warbands of earlier generations.

Barov also established the first royal laws governing the kingdom, known as the **Edicts of Morov**, which formalized the authority of the crown and defined the rights and responsibilities of the noble houses. Under his leadership the Kingdom of Damara expanded both economically and militarily, becoming a formidable regional power.

By the time **Vladimir Von Zarovich** ascended to the throne, Damara had entered a period of relative stability. Unlike his warrior ancestors, Vladimir possessed a deep interest in scholarship and philosophy. His reign marked a significant cultural transformation within the kingdom. Vladimir encouraged learning and the preservation of knowledge from earlier civilizations, particularly the arcane lore scattered across the ruins of Netheril. 

Several major institutions were founded during his rule:
- **The Academy of Morov**, devoted to philosophy, astronomy, and history  
- **The Royal Archives of Damara**, which preserved ancient texts and records  
- **Scholarly guilds** dedicated to engineering, medicine, and navigation  

Trade with southern regions increased dramatically during this time, bringing new ideas and cultural influences into the kingdom. Under Vladimir’s guidance Damara entered what later historians would describe as **The Era of Knowledge**, a time of intellectual growth and artistic development. Yet the shadows of earlier ages were never far away. Many scholars investigating ancient Netherese ruins reported strange magical disturbances within the **Giantspire Mountains**, remnants of forces unleashed during the fall of Netheril. Though largely dismissed at the time as lingering magical anomalies, these disturbances would later prove to be connected to far darker powers buried deep beneath the mountains.



## The Founding Time (1 DR to 300 DR)

The year **1 DR** marks the beginning of the **Dalereckoning calendar**, the system of timekeeping that would eventually spread across much of Faerûn. This era is remembered as the **Founding Time**, a period when the scattered survivors of ancient empires began rebuilding civilization after centuries of chaos following the Fall of Netheril. Human kingdoms rose across the continent, trade routes expanded, and alliances were forged between the emerging races of the Realms. It was during this time that the political landscape of Faerûn began to resemble the world known today.


### 75 DR — Founding of the Order of the Yellow Rose

In the early years of the **Dalereckoning calendar**, when the young nations of Faerûn were still forming from the ashes of the fallen Netherese empire, a secret order was established in the eastern lands that would later become **Damara**. This brotherhood became known as the **Order of the Yellow Rose**. The order was founded with a single sacred mandate - “To safeguard the Keys and ensure that **Tharizdun** is never awakened.” 

Its creation was tied directly to events that had occurred nearly two centuries earlier, when the terrible avatar of the Chained God—**Shorthagot**—had been defeated and imprisoned by a band of mortal champions at the end of the **Netherese Reckoning**. To ensure the prison could never be easily broken, the magic binding Shorthagot had been divided into **four powerful relics**, later known as the **Keys of Shorthagot**. Each key was entrusted to one of the champions and hidden in distant lands. One of these relics was carried east into the mountains of Damara.

The champion who carried the key into Damara was **Saint Aurellan Dawnward**, a legendary paladin and devoted servant of **Torm**, god of duty and righteous courage. Aurellan had fought in the final battle against Shorthagot and helped seal the avatar within a prison tied to the **Shadowfell**. Understanding the danger the Keys represented, he vowed to dedicate his remaining life to ensuring the prison would never be undone. Traveling to the remote mountains that would one day overlook the city of **Valls**, Aurellan founded a monastery dedicated to vigilance and sacrifice. From this sanctuary he began training a new order of warriors and scholars—monks, knights, and priests bound by sacred oath to guard the Key entrusted to him. Thus was born the **Order of the Yellow Rose**.

Unknown to most of the world, the monastery was constructed atop the ancient Netherese ruins of **Thul Avenar**, a forgotten research complex built during the height of Netheril’s magical experimentation. Deep beneath the monastery lay an ancient **planar gateway**, a relic of Netherese arcane engineering. This gateway opened to a hidden demi-plane aligned with the **Plane of Shadow**, a place where fragments of forbidden Netherese magic had been sealed away after the fall of the empire. Within that shadowed realm rested the mysterious construct known as the **Infernal Machine**, a device created by Netherese arcanists in their attempts to harness **Primordial Aether**, a force older than the Weave itself. The prison of Shorthagot was bound to this hidden realm. The Order of the Yellow Rose therefore guarded not only the **Key** entrusted to them, but also the gateway that could lead to the prison itself.

The Order of the Yellow Rose developed a unique philosophy that blended martial discipline, monastic asceticism, and sacred duty. Members of the order swore vows of **silence, poverty, and eternal vigilance**. They believed that protecting the world from cosmic threats required both strength of body and clarity of mind. Unlike many knightly orders, the monks of the Yellow Rose believed that **knowledge itself was a weapon**. They preserved fragments of ancient lore passed down from the days of Netheril, studying forbidden histories so they might better recognize the signs of rising darkness.

After many years guiding the order, **Saint Aurellan Dawnward** eventually died within the monastery he had founded. His body was interred in sacred catacombs beneath the **Citadel of the Yellow Rose**, deep within the mountain stronghold overlooking Valls. According to the order’s secret traditions, the **Key of Shorthagot** entrusted to Aurellan was sealed within those same catacombs, protected by powerful divine wards and hidden knowledge known only to the highest members of the order. For more than a millennium, the Order of the Yellow Rose stood watch over this terrible secret—guarding the Key, the gateway beneath the monastery, and the ancient prison where the essence of Shorthagot remained bound. Few beyond the order ever knew the true purpose of their vigil. Fewer still understood what horrors might return should that vigilance ever fail.


### The Standing Stone and the Birth of Dalereckoning

The Dalereckoning calendar begins with the raising of the **Standing Stone** in the region that would become known as the **Dalelands**. For generations, humans migrating east from the fallen lands of Netheril had settled among the forests of Cormanthor, lands traditionally held by the elves. Tensions between the two peoples grew as human settlements expanded.Rather than allow war to erupt, the elven rulers of Cormanthor and the human settlers forged a historic agreement. In the year **1 DR**, the elves raised the Standing Stone as a symbol of peace between their people and the human settlers. The agreement established several important principles:

- Humans would be permitted to settle the open lands surrounding the great forest.
- The deep woods of **Cormanthor** would remain sacred elven territory.
- Trade and travel between the two peoples would remain open and peaceful.

The year of this agreement became the starting point of **Dalereckoning**, a calendar that gradually spread across much of Faerûn and remains the most widely used system of dating events. Following the collapse of Netheril centuries earlier, humanity had been scattered into small tribal societies. During the Founding Time, these groups began forming organized kingdoms and city-states. Many early human realms were built atop or near the ruins of older civilizations.

- **The Dalelands**: Small farming communities and independent towns flourished in the fertile lands surrounding the forest of Cormanthor. These settlements would eventually grow into the famous Dales, including Shadowdale, Mistledale, and Battledale. The Dales developed a culture centered around independence and cooperation. Rather than forming a centralized kingdom, each Dale governed itself while maintaining alliances with neighboring communities.
- **The Sword Coast**: Along the western shores of Faerûn, trade ports began forming along the coast as merchants traveled between northern and southern markets. These early coastal settlements laid the foundations for some of the greatest cities of the Realms. One of the most important settlements of the Founding Time was **Waterdeep**. Originally little more than a fortified trading post located along a natural harbor beneath Mount Waterdeep, the settlement quickly grew into a vital trade hub. Merchants from across Faerûn arrived seeking goods from distant lands, including dwarven metals, elven crafts, and exotic products from the southern kingdoms. Although still small compared to its later glory, Waterdeep was already becoming known as **the City of Splendors**.  While the Dalelands and Sword Coast developed as centers of trade and settlement, other regions of Faerûn saw the rise of early kingdoms.
- **Cormyr**: Though not fully established until later centuries, the early tribes that would form the Kingdom of Cormyr began consolidating power in the forests west of the Dalelands Cormyr would eventually become one of the most stable and influential human kingdoms in Faerûn.
- **Chondath**: In the southern lands around the Vilhon Reach, powerful merchant city-states began forming alliances that would eventually grow into the nation of Chondath. This region became an important trade center linking inland Faerûn to the Sea of Fallen Stars. The societies that emerged during the Founding Time were far less magically dominated than the fallen empire of Netheril. Without powerful archwizards ruling from floating cities, leadership fell instead to kings, councils, and merchant guilds.
- **Local Lords and Nobles**: Many regions were governed by hereditary rulers who controlled small territories.
- **Merchant Councils**: In major trade settlements, wealthy merchants often held political authority.
- **Independent Townships**: Especially in the Dalelands, communities governed themselves through councils of elders or elected leaders.
- **Damaran Empire**: The raising of the **Standing Stone** in the Dalelands marks the beginning of the **Dalereckoning calendar**, a system of timekeeping that gradually spreads across much of Faerûn. During this era the Kingdom of **Damara**, still under the steady rule of the **Von Zarovich dynasty**, enjoys a period of prosperity and relative peace. Trade routes expand along the eastern plains and through the mountain passes of the **Galena** and **Giantspire Mountains**. Settlements across the realm grow into fortified towns and thriving market cities. Merchants from southern lands bring new knowledge, crafts, and technologies, allowing Damara to develop into a stable and influential northern kingdom. In 300 DR — The Age of Conflict began in Damara.. The stability of the Von Zarovich realm begins to fracture under the harsh rule of **Lord Ivan Von Zarovich**. Ivan governs through fear and absolute authority, demanding strict loyalty from the noble houses and enforcing heavy taxation to maintain a massive standing army. His policies provoke widespread unrest throughout the kingdom. Several provinces rise in open rebellion, and border regions threaten secession. Ivan responds with ruthless efficiency, deploying his armies to crush dissent wherever it appears. Entire towns are burned and rebellious lords executed in public displays meant to reinforce the power of the crown. Though Ivan succeeds in preserving the unity of Damara, his brutal reign leaves deep resentment among the noble houses—tensions that will continue to shape the politics of the realm for centuries to come.

The collapse of Netheril had left many dangerous ruins scattered across Faerûn, and monsters often emerged from these forgotten places. Local militias and adventuring bands became common as settlements sought protection from threats. Legal systems during this era varied widely between regions but generally emphasized:
- Protection of trade routes
- Defense against raiders and monsters
- Agreements between neighboring settlements

### Religion and the Return of the Gods

Following the turmoil of earlier ages, religious institutions regained prominence during the Founding Time. Temples dedicated to gods such as **Mystra**, **Tyr**, **Chauntea**, and **Lathander** appeared across human lands. These temples served not only as centers of worship but also as places of learning, healing, and community organization. Clerics and paladins often acted as mediators between rival settlements and defenders against supernatural threats. By the end of this era, the foundations of modern Faerûn had been established.
Trade routes linked distant regions of the continent. Human kingdoms had begun replacing the shattered remnants of older empires. New cities rose from the ruins of the past. Yet beneath the growing prosperity of this age, the shadows of earlier civilizations still lingered. Across the world, the buried ruins of Netheril and older empires remained filled with ancient magic and forgotten machines. Most believed these relics to be nothing more than dangerous curiosities of a lost age. But some scholars, cultists, and secret orders continued searching the ruins for knowledge long believed destroyed. For within those forgotten places lay the remnants of powers that had once nearly reshaped the world.


## The Rise of the North (300 DR to 1000 DR)

Following the stability established during the Founding Time, the northern regions of Faerûn entered a long era of expansion, conflict, and political upheaval. From the forests of the Dalelands to the storm-lashed harbors of the Sword Coast, kingdoms rose and fell with remarkable speed. Trade flourished along the coasts and rivers of the north, yet the region remained plagued by constant threats from monsters, raiders, and ambitious warlords. This age became known as **The Rise of the North**, a period in which the foundations of many of Faerûn’s most influential cities and cultures were established.

During this period the **Sword Coast** transformed from a scattered collection of fishing villages and trading posts into one of the most economically important regions of Faerûn. 
Merchants from across the continent began using the coastal routes linking northern and southern markets. Ships carried grain, timber, metals, and magical goods between distant ports. Several settlements grew rapidly into powerful city-states.

Founded by the legendary explorer **Balduran**, Baldur’s Gate began as a fortified harbor along the River Chionthar. Balduran's voyages brought immense wealth to the settlement, and the city quickly expanded into a major trade hub linking inland Faerûn to the Sea of Swords. Baldur’s Gate developed a unique system of governance. Rather than a king, the city was ruled by the **Council of Four**, powerful dukes chosen from among the city’s wealthiest and most influential families. These rulers controlled trade policy, defense, and diplomatic relations with neighboring powers. Despite its wealth, Baldur’s Gate developed a reputation as a dangerous and politically volatile city.

Further north, the city of **Neverwinter** rose along the warm waters flowing from Mount Hotenow. Unlike most northern settlements, Neverwinter enjoyed a temperate climate due to geothermal heat beneath the land. This allowed crops to grow year-round, giving the city both economic stability and strategic importance. Neverwinter was ruled by a noble line of kings who maintained strong alliances with surrounding settlements and dwarven clans in the nearby mountains. Neverwinter would later earn the title **“The Jewel of the North.”**

The Age of Conflict in Damara started around 300 DR. While the western coast prospered through trade, the eastern kingdom of **Damara** entered a period of turmoil. Under the harsh rule of **Lord Ivan Von Zarovich**, internal tensions within the kingdom erupted into open rebellion. Ivan ruled through fear and uncompromising military authority, demanding strict loyalty from the noble houses and imposing heavy taxation to maintain a powerful standing army. Several provinces rose against his rule, and border regions threatened secession. Ivan responded with brutal efficiency. Rebel leaders were executed, entire towns were razed, and dissent was crushed wherever it appeared. Though his ruthless campaigns preserved the unity of Damara, the scars of his rule deepened political divisions among the kingdom’s noble houses. These tensions would shape Damaran politics for centuries to come.

At the northernmost edge of the Sword Coast stood **Luskan**, a harsh and dangerous port city dominated by pirate fleets and mercenary captains. Luskan’s government was notoriously unstable. The city was controlled by powerful maritime warlords known as **High Captains**, each commanding their own fleets and territory within the harbor. Political alliances within Luskan shifted constantly, and power was often decided by naval strength rather than diplomacy. Despite its lawless reputation, Luskan became an important gateway for trade with the far northern lands. 

Religion played an increasingly important role during this period. Temples across the north gained political influence as priests provided healing, guidance, and protection against supernatural threats. One of the most powerful and feared religious movements during this era was the growing influence of the **Church of Bane**. Bane, the god of tyranny and conquest, attracted followers among warlords, generals, and rulers who sought to dominate their rivals through strength and fear. The church spread rapidly through military organizations and mercenary companies, often supporting leaders who promised to expand their influence. In several regions the priests of Bane held nearly as much power as kings. The ideological conflict between these faiths shaped many political struggles of the era. Opposing them were faiths devoted to gods of justice and protection, including: 
- **Tyr**, god of justice
- **Helm**, god of guardianship
- **Torm**, god of duty and loyalty

One of the most devastating conflicts of this period was the series of battles known as the **Orcgate Wars**. These wars erupted when massive orc hordes descended from the Spine of the World and other northern mountain ranges, launching coordinated invasions against human and dwarven settlements. Entire kingdoms were destroyed as the orc armies swept through frontier regions. Human rulers formed temporary alliances with dwarven clans and elven forces in order to repel the invasions. Though the orcs were eventually driven back into the mountains, the wars left large regions of the north depopulated and vulnerable to further threats.

Life in the North remained harsh even during periods of peace. Settlements were often isolated, surrounded by wilderness filled with monsters, ancient ruins, and hostile tribes. Communities relied heavily on local militias and adventurers for protection. Professional soldiers became common, selling their services to cities and nobles.Many individuals sought fortune exploring ancient ruins left behind by earlier civilizations such as Netheril. These explorers often returned with magical relics or stories of forgotten horrors buried beneath the earth.

By the end of the first millennium Dalereckoning, the northern regions of Faerûn had become one of the most politically dynamic areas of the world. The Sword Coast was now home to powerful trading cities. The Dalelands remained fiercely independent. New kingdoms continued to rise across the continent. Yet beneath the growing civilization of the north, ancient forces stirred. The ruins of Netheril still dotted the land. Lost magical relics resurfaced in the hands of ambitious rulers and reckless scholars. And in the forgotten corners of Faerûn, cults devoted to darker powers continued their quiet work—searching for knowledge buried since the fall of empires. The age of rising kingdoms had only begun.


### The rise and fall of Von Zarovich Family

By the beginning of the second millennium Dalereckoning, the Kingdom of Damara had become a hardened military power. Much of this transformation was driven by the rule of **Lord Barov II Von Zarovich**, a stern and uncompromising ruler who believed the survival of the realm depended upon strength and expansion. Barov II reorganized Damara’s armies into disciplined standing forces and fortified the kingdom’s borders with new citadels and military roads. Campaigns were launched against rival territories and raiding tribes across the eastern plains and the foothills of the **Galena** and **Giantspire Mountains**. Under Barov II’s command, Damara expanded its influence across neighboring regions. Though these campaigns brought wealth and territory, they also transformed Damara into a heavily militarized state where loyalty to the crown and service in the army became central pillars of society. It was during this era that the Von Zarovich dynasty reached the height of its worldly power.

In the year **1090 DR**, **Strahd Von Zarovich** was born to **Lord Barov II** and **Queen Ravenovia Van Roeyen**. From an early age Strahd was raised within the rigid discipline of Damara’s military culture. He was educated in the arts of warfare, statecraft, and strategy, quickly proving himself to be a brilliant commander and tactician. Many nobles believed Strahd was destined to inherit not only his father’s throne but also the legacy of conquest that had defined the dynasty. Few could have foreseen the darker path his life would ultimately take.

As a young general, Strahd led several successful campaigns to expand Damara’s influence beyond its traditional borders. One such campaign brought him to a remote and fertile valley nestled among the mountains, a region rumored to contain ancient ruins dating back to the time of Netheril. Strahd conquered the valley and named it **Barovia**, honoring his father **Barov II**. Seeing both strategic and personal value in the land, he ordered the construction of a mighty fortress overlooking the valley. This castle—later known as **Castle Ravenloft**—was named in tribute to his beloved mother, **Queen Ravenovia**. With the conquest of Barovia and the construction of Ravenloft, the Von Zarovich dynasty appeared to have reached the pinnacle of its dominance.

### The Curse of Strahd

The triumph of Barovia would soon turn to tragedy. During the wedding celebration of Strahd’s younger brother **Sergei Von Zarovich** and the noblewoman **Tatyana**, Strahd was consumed by jealousy and bitterness. In a moment that would change history, he murdered Sergei in an act of betrayal. Seeking power and eternal life, Strahd had already made a dark pact with a mysterious and malevolent entity. The pact transformed him into the first of a new kind of creature—**a vampire lord bound by dark magic and eternal hunger**. The land itself seemed to recoil from the act. Strange mists descended upon Barovia, sealing the valley away from the rest of the world. Barovia vanished into a supernatural demiplane of dread, forever isolated within the **Mists of Ravenloft**. With Strahd trapped within his cursed domain, the power of the Von Zarovich dynasty was suddenly shattered.

In the centuries following Strahd’s disappearance, Damara fell into a period of political collapse. Without the authority of the Von Zarovich line, rival noble houses began competing for control of the kingdom. Alliances fractured and local warlords seized power across the region. This chaotic period became known to historians as **The Fractured Realms**. Civil wars erupted across Damara’s territories, weakening the kingdom and leaving it vulnerable to raiders, monsters, and external threats. Trade routes fell into disrepair and many cities declined as regional powers struggled for dominance. For nearly a century, Damara remained divided and unstable.



### The Rise of the Frostmantle Dynasty

The fractured kingdom was finally reunified under the leadership of **Irik Frostmantle** in 1285 DR, a powerful warlord from the northern tribes. Through a combination of military strength and political alliances, Frostmantle defeated the rival noble houses and restored central authority over the kingdom. Crucial to his victory was the support of **House Williams**, a powerful noble family whose influence helped legitimize Frostmantle’s claim to rule. Irik crowned himself King of Damara, establishing the **Frostmantle Dynasty**, which would shape the kingdom’s future for centuries to come.

The early Frostmantle rulers were deeply wary of arcane power. Memories of the catastrophic fall of Netheril—and rumors of dangerous magic hidden within ancient ruins—led the Frostmantle kings to impose strict laws regulating the use of magic throughout Damara. This era became known as the **Cleansing Wars**. Arcane practitioners were heavily regulated, persecuted, or driven into hiding. Many wizards fled the kingdom, while others were executed or forced to swear loyalty to the crown. Magical artifacts were confiscated and destroyed, and several ancient ruins were sealed or placed under royal guard. Though harsh, these policies reshaped Damaran society into one defined by martial strength, suspicion of arcane power, and strict royal authority.



## The Time of Troubles (1358 DR)

In the year **1358 DR**, the balance of the cosmos was shattered when **Ao the Overgod** cast the gods of Faerûn down from the heavens and forced them to walk the world in mortal form. This cataclysmic event became known as **The Time of Troubles**. The crisis began when the **Tablets of Fate**, ancient artifacts that defined the relationship between the gods and mortal reality, were stolen by the deities **Bane** and **Myrkul**. In response to this act of divine defiance, Ao stripped the gods of their divinity and exiled them to Toril until the Tablets were recovered.

During this period, gods walked Faerûn as mortals. Their presence unleashed chaos across the world as divine rivalries spilled into the material plane. Cities burned, cults rose in sudden fervor, and mortals found themselves entangled in conflicts between beings they had once worshipped from afar. Several gods perished during these events. **Bane**, **Bhaal**, and **Myrkul**—later remembered as the **Dead Three**—were slain during the struggle for power. At the same time, new gods rose to take their places. The most profound change came with the death of **Mystra**, the goddess of magic. When she was slain while attempting to reach Ao’s celestial realm, the **Weave**, the invisible structure that governs magic throughout Faerûn, collapsed temporarily. Arcane magic surged wildly across the world, causing devastating magical catastrophes.

Mystra was later reborn through the mortal mage **Midnight**, who ascended to become the new goddess of magic. With the Tablets of Fate restored, Ao returned the surviving gods to the heavens. Although the crisis ended, the **Time of Troubles permanently altered the nature of divine power in Faerûn**. The relationship between gods and mortals changed forever, and the pantheon of Faerûn was reshaped in ways that would echo through the centuries to come.

In the chaotic aftermath of the **Time of Troubles**, when the boundaries between the mortal world and the divine had been violently disturbed, darker forces began to stir in the shadows of Faerûn. Secret cults devoted to **Tharizdun, the Chained God**, exploited the instability left behind by the divine crisis. These cultists sought ancient sources of power buried since the fall of **Netheril**, uncovering fragments of forbidden knowledge connected to a mysterious arcane construct known as the **Infernal Machine**—a device tied to primordial forces older than the Weave itself. 

Through these artifacts, the cults attempted to awaken **Shorthagot**, a terrible avatar of Tharizdun whose presence threatened to bring ruin to the eastern lands. As the cult’s power grew, a band of champions rose to oppose them. Drawing upon ancient knowledge and powerful relics, they confronted Shorthagot before the entity could fully manifest in the material world. After a catastrophic battle, the champions succeeded in imprisoning Shorthagot within the **Shadowfell**, sealing the avatar away before it could break free into Faerûn. To ensure the prison could never easily be undone, the magic that bound Shorthagot was divided into **four powerful relics**, later known as the **Keys of Shorthagot**. These artifacts were scattered and hidden across Damara and distant lands so that no single faction could ever release the imprisoned entity. Though the champions believed the threat contained, whispers of the Keys and the power they hold have endured in hidden texts, secret orders, and forbidden cults ever since. 



## The Spellplague (1385 DR)

In **1385 DR**, Faerûn was struck by one of the most devastating magical catastrophes in recorded history: **The Spellplague**. The disaster began when the god **Cyric**, manipulated by the dark goddess **Shar**, murdered **Mystra**, the goddess of magic. With Mystra’s death, the **Weave**—the invisible structure that governed arcane magic throughout the world—collapsed. Without the stabilizing presence of the Weave, raw magical energy erupted across Toril in the form of destructive **blue fire**. Entire cities vanished in moments, landscapes twisted into unnatural forms, and creatures touched by the magical chaos were warped into strange new beings. Across Faerûn, spells failed unpredictably or produced catastrophic effects. Wizards found their magic unreliable, and many magical traditions were lost as centuries of accumulated arcane knowledge suddenly became dangerous or useless.

The disaster did not affect only magic. The boundaries between worlds were violently disrupted. Portions of the long-lost twin world **Abeir** merged with **Toril**, reshaping entire continents. Regions disappeared while others were replaced by alien lands and creatures never before seen on Faerûn. The **Underdark** suffered immense devastation as caverns collapsed and subterranean ecosystems were destroyed. Many ancient tunnels and cities were lost forever beneath shifting stone. For nearly a century, Faerûn struggled to survive in the aftermath of the Spellplague. Civilization fractured, magical institutions collapsed, and scholars struggled to understand the new and unstable nature of arcane power. Though magic would eventually stabilize, the Spellplague permanently reshaped the world, leaving scars on both the land and the history of Faerûn that would endure for generations.



## The Sundering (1400 DR to 1490 DR)

During the early fifteenth century Dalereckoning, the world endured another period of upheaval known as **The Sundering**. Through the actions of the **Chosen of the Gods**, the worlds of **Toril** and **Abeir**, which had partially merged during the Spellplague, were gradually separated once again. As divine balance returned, the **Weave of magic was restored**, stabilizing arcane power across Faerûn. The gods reclaimed their celestial dominions, and the chaotic magical conditions that had plagued the world for nearly a century began to subside. For many kingdoms, this era marked the beginning of recovery and renewal. Yet in the eastern lands of **Damara**, darker forces quietly took advantage of the instability left behind by the Spellplague and the Sundering.

During the early decades following the Sundering, **Damara entered a period of political turmoil** known as **The Great Unrest**. Old rivalries between noble houses resurfaced as weakened royal authority struggled to maintain unity across the realm. Trade routes faltered, border conflicts intensified, and political factions within the kingdom maneuvered for greater influence. Among these houses, **House Williams** rose rapidly in power. Through careful alliances, political cunning, and manipulation of court intrigue, the house secured key positions within Damara’s government. Unknown to most of the realm, darker forces had already begun to infiltrate the house, subtly guiding its rise to power.

In **1475 DR**, the noble **Lord Edmund Williams** married a mysterious and striking woman known as **Lady Elysra Nivariel Williams**. To the outside world she appeared as a charismatic and intelligent noblewoman whose influence strengthened the political position of House Williams. In truth, Lady Elysra was a powerful **succubus operating in mortal disguise**, secretly serving the will of **Tharizdun**, the Chained God. Working from the shadows, she began carefully orchestrating alliances, rivalries, and betrayals among Damara’s ruling houses. Through subtle manipulation and long-planned intrigue, she gradually weakened the kingdom’s political stability while positioning her agents in key positions of influence.

Prince Ian Frostmantle is severely wounded in battle while investigating Valls and trying to reach Mistress Allyson of the Yellow Rose in Valls. 

### The Fall of Valls
In **1496 DR**, Lady Elysra’s plans reached a catastrophic turning point. The city of **Valls**, home to a major **citadel of the Order of the Yellow Rose**, had long guarded secrets connected to ancient powers buried beneath the city. Under Elysra’s command, cultists and corrupted allies launched a devastating assault against the citadel. During the battle, her followers succeeded in breaching a hidden **planar gateway beneath Valls**, a gateway tied to the **Shadowfell**. When the seal was broken, dark energies poured into the region. Creatures from the Shadowfell crossed into the material world, and the city was overwhelmed by supernatural horrors. The fall of Valls marked one of the darkest moments in Damaran history since the fragmentation of the kingdom centuries earlier.
